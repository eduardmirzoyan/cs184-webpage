Overview

This assignment simulates cloth physics by generating a rectangular grid that is composed of many point masses and springs. As we’ve learnt in class, the physical model of springs and its forces can be leveraged to create meshes that can be displaced in the presence of forces. We begin by first creating the cloth, then we use a numerical integration technique to allow our cloth to fall in response to gravity. Then, we add a layer of complexity that deals with collisions if our cloth were to interact with other meshes. We finally wrap it up by accounting for self collisions to prevent uncanny visual artifacts. Through implementing different types of shaders in GLSL, we provide an extra level of visual appeal to our simulation.

Part 1: Masses and springs

In the first part of the project we build a grid of masses and springs in order to be able to simulate physics on our piece of cloth. Given a sheet of cloth with some desired dimensions and parameters, we divided the cloth up into evenly spaced point masses and then connected nearby masses with springs.

Part 2: Simulation via numerical integration

In this part, we implemented basic simulation of the cloth over time. Most of the logic is simply translating common physics formulas like F=ma into code. We were able to define physical constants like density, spring constant ks, and dampening in order to emulate the effects of cloth in the real world. We find the external forces on each point mass on the cloth and use Verlet integration to compute the new point mass positions of our cloth.

This is the control case of the cloth we will use to compare the effects of changing it’s different properties.

Density

As we alter the density, the feeling of weight of the cloth changes. As density increases, the cloth is more saggy in the folded areas and is more straight in the less weighted areas. This gives the illusion that the cloth is has more mass. As we decrease the density, we can see the cloth become more loose and silky. More wrinkles can be seen since they aren’t affected by gravity as much.

KS

The spring constant ks affects the tension between the different sections of the cloth. As ks increases, we can cloth become more like a sheet of metal and not stretch between pinned points. This is also less rippling while the cloth is falling since it’s more stiff. As ks decreases, the cloth becomes more saggy and will tend to stretch more as it falls.

Damping

The damping of the cloth influences the amount of swinging and rippling as it falls. Most of the effects of damping are seen while the cloth is moving rather than when it’s still. As damping increases, we can see the cloth fall as if it is in low gravity. It will tend to swing very little and reach it’s static state much quicker. As damping decreases, we can see the cloth ripple internally and swing dramatically as it falls. We can even see it start to swing in the opposite direction it was dropped multiple times before it comes to a still.

Part 3: Handling collisions with other objects

This section focused on handling collision with other objects in our scene. We want our cloth to wrap around any sphere it falls on to and make sure it doesn’t pass through them. We can simply check if any of the point masses intersect with any external object and simply ‘bump’ the position of the point mass up to the object’s surface. We needed to implement the logic of a collision for a sphere and a plane.

As you can see, as the ks changes, the final resting state of the cloth changes as it interacts with the sphere. As ks is smaller, the cloth sags and wraps around the sphere more. The cloth is more loose. As ks is larger, the cloth acts more stiff and spread out on the sphere instead of wrapping around.

Part 4: Handling self-collisions

This section focused on developing the capability of the cloth to collide with itself. When we work with clothing we often expect it to overlap with itself and we should see real-world results when this happens. Instead of naively looping though all the pairs of point masses, we used spatial hashing to build a table to maps values to Vectors of point masses. We can use this table as a look up when looping though point masses and apply a repulsive collision force to any pair of masses that are too close to each other.

This is the control case for the observation of self collisions in the falling piece of cloth.

As we increase the density of the cloth, we can see the fabric falling at a much faster rate. Less bouncing is observed since the cloth is rendered to be ‘heavier’ and therefor affected by gravity more. When the density is decreased, the cloth almost behaves in slow motion, with more exaggerated bounces.

When we tweak the ks of the cloth, the amount of ripples and waves in the cloth change. When ks is low, the cloth is very lively especially when its mostly on the plane. It will vibrate for a long time before coming to a rest. When ks is large, the fabric acts as a sheet of paper rather than cloth.

Part 5: Shaders

A shader program is a program that runs on the GPU to perform shading. It has two primary stages: the vertex stage and the fragment stage. Since meshes can be represented as a collection of many vertices connected in such a way that describes the shape of an object, the vertex shader takes individual vertices and can perform any sort of computation on the vertex. This stage is typically used to pipeline information such as the vertex’s normal or position into the fragment shader stage. The fragment shader stage utilizes the data provided by the vertex stages to perform all sorts of shading, lighting, shadow mapping, and texture mapping, as shown below.

A classic shading model that is easily implementable in GLSL is the Blinn-Phong shading model. In essence, the Blinn-Phong shading model takes an object’s diffuse, ambient, and specular properties and calculates the amount of light each property emits. It then adds the light contribution from each component to produce a shaded mesh.
