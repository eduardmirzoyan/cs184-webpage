Overview

This assignment was divided into two sections. The first section consisted of working de Casteljau’s algorithm to create easily malleable curves in a 2D and 3D space. This process involved using the idea of linear interpolation between points in order to generate the next proper point on the final curve. The second section worked heavily with rendering and manipulating 3D meshes. This section began with the calculation of polygon normals in order to generate a phong shading scheme, and later ended with the ability to edit our 3D meshes. The editing operations we implemented were edge flipping, splitting and mesh upsampling.

Part 1 - Bezier Curves with 1D de Casteljau Subdivision

The de Casteljau algorithm estimates a Bezier curve by calculating a linear interpolation (lerp) between consecutive pairs of points in a given set. This step is repeated recursively until there is only one point left in the set. This point corresponds to the location of the curve at time t.

Part 2 - Bezier Surfaces with Separable 1D de Casteljau

The de Casteljau algorithm can extend to Bezier surfaces by simply lerping with 3D point instead of 2D. We simply incorporated linearly interpolating the z coordinating as well in the calculations and were left with a 3D Bezier patch. The rest of algorithm was the same as in Part 1.

Part 3 - Area Weighted Normal Vertex

We implemented area-weighted vertex normals by doing the following:

  1. Given a vertex V’s half edge, iterate over each of its neighboring polygons
    1. Calculate the area of the polygon
    1. Get the normal of the polygon
    1. Multiply the normal by the area
    1. Add the weighted normal to V’s normal
  1. Normalize V’s normal
  1. Return V’s normal

Part 4 - Edge Flip

Edge flipping is a fairly straight forward process which we implemented by doing the following:

  1. Compile a list of all the edges, vertices and faces involved in the flip
  1. Discard any cases with boundary edges since they shouldn’t be flipped
  1. Now we just need to reassign the neighbors of our chosen half-edges
  1. Then we make sure that all our edge’s values stay consistent with our new neighbors
    1. Reassign the proper twins, but only for the outer edges
    1. Reassign vertices with their proper half edges

Part 5 - Edge Split

Since we did not do the extra credit, we implemented edge splitting by doing the following:

  1. Compile a list of all the edges, vertices and faces involved in the split
  1. Discard any cases with boundary edges since they don’t need to flipped
  1. Create a new vertex that is the halfway point between edge that needs to be split
  1. Create the new faces and edges that will be spawned from performing the edge split
  1. Assign these new elements their correct neighbors post split
  1. Now update the neighbors of existing elements that were affected by split

Part 6 - Loop Subdivision for Mesh Upsampling

We implemented loop subdivision by first precomputing the positions of the new vertices and then storing them in the class member variables. This is preferable to first splitting the edges and then calculating the positions, since if the position computation can be done ahead of time pre-element adding, it should. Then, we split the non-boundary edges, update the position of the vertex returned from the edge split. Following that, we flip any vertices that are connected to both an old and a new vertex, and finally we update the positions of all our old vertices.

In the images of the four cows above, note that the first row is a plain cow.dae with no subdivision performed, while the row below represents the same model with one level of subdivision. The differences between figure 6.2 and 6.4 are already enormous. Figure 6.4 exhibits a much smoother surface throughout the mesh as a whole.

Although it might not be apparent, what this implies is that sharp corners and edges are also smoothened out. This can be undesirable, depending on what sort of mesh you’re trying to render! Another issue with sharp corners and edges is that a simple mesh may transform into a shape that initially seems unexpected. One reason for this is that such mesh may not have enough edges before subdivision has been performed. We demonstrate this vividly with the figures below.

The effects that loop subdivision has on meshes that are not only close to uniform in proportion but sharp as well is no more apparent than in the cube.dae. Figure 6.5 to figure 6.9 illustrates the journey of an unwitting cube being relentlessly subdivided until it’s nothing but an echo of its former self. What sticks out through this metamorphosis is how our once symmetric shape is now grossly asymmetric. The reasoning for this might not be apparent at first, until we recall what each face of the cube in figure 6.5 are comprised of: two triangles!

“Ah, so one reason for the cube’s asymmetry is because the holistic composition of its faces is asymmetric in nature. An answer to this is to first enforce symmetry on each face by first splitting the edge on each cube, and then perform loop subdivision!
